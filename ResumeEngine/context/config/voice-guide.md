# Voice Guide — Eric Manchester Professional Writing Style

Extracted from Career_History_Complete.md and established context files. This guide ensures consistent voice and tone across all resumes, applications, and professional communications.

## Core Voice Characteristics

### Specificity First
- **Pattern:** Concrete facts, quantified metrics, specific examples
- **Example:** "Generated 7M+ personalized video variants with AI-synthesized narration" (NOT "created many video variants")
- **Why:** Demonstrates credibility through precision; vague claims undermine expertise

### Metric-Driven
- **Pattern:** Lead with numbers when available; always support claims with scale/scope
- **Examples:**
  - "Increased ad revenue 350% through VOD automation"
  - "23.5 billion streaming minutes during Paris 2024 Olympics"
  - "Emmy-winning interactive experience serving millions of concurrent users"
  - "World-record concurrent streaming (5M+ simultaneous viewers)"
- **Why:** Numbers validate accomplishment better than adjectives

### Technical Depth
- **Pattern:** Use precise technical terminology; explain architecture and methodology
- **Examples:**
  - "Frame-accurate overlay system" (NOT "interactive feature")
  - "Asynchronous workflow orchestration" (NOT "improved process")
  - "Server-side ad insertion (SSAI) with dynamic decisioning" (NOT "ad technology")
  - "SAG-AFTRA compliant AI voice synthesis" (NOT "voice generation")
- **Why:** Shows deep technical understanding; differentiates from non-technical professionals

### Direct and Unadorned
- **Pattern:** Active voice, clear sentence structure, no filler
- **Examples:**
  - ✓ "Architected Live Actions from ground up"
  - ✗ "Spearheaded the revolutionary Live Actions initiative"
  - ✓ "Designed content workflows for Olympic Daily Recap"
  - ✗ "Leveraged cutting-edge technology to create impactful recap features"
- **Why:** Direct language reads as confident; filler reads as uncertain

### Individual Accountability
- **Pattern:** Use "I" when sole responsibility; clarify team context when applicable
- **Examples:**
  - "I architected and built Peacock Live Actions from the ground up"
  - "I designed content workflows; team built underlying AI voice platform"
  - "I mentored junior TPM to principal role"
- **Why:** Clear accountability demonstrates confidence and precision

### Distinction-Focused
- **Pattern:** Clarify scope of work; distinguish individual vs. team contributions
- **Examples from Paris Olympics Emmy:**
  - What Eric built: "Live Actions (end-to-end), Olympic Daily Recap content workflows"
  - What team built: "AI voice synthesis platform, Discovery Multiview, broader infrastructure"
- **Why:** Emmy credit lists "Eric Manchester, Software Developer — sole person in role category"

### Outcome-Oriented
- **Pattern:** Lead with business impact; explain technical implementation as support
- **Structure:** [Impact] achieved through [methodology] using [technical approach]
- **Example:** "Increased ad revenue 200%+ through interactive advertising features (Live Actions, pause ads) using SCTE-35 triggering and SAM/FreeWheel integration"
- **Why:** Business impact demonstrates value; technical details validate feasibility

### Award-Focused
- **Pattern:** Link technical work to industry recognition when applicable
- **Examples:**
  - "Technology & Engineering Emmy (2023) for Key Plays AI/ML feature"
  - "Outstanding Interactive Experience Emmy (2025) for Paris 2024 Olympics"
  - "Emmy Award (2005) for pioneering world-record concurrent live streaming"
- **Why:** Emmy validation provides third-party credibility

## Tone Guidelines

### Professional Tone
- Confident without arrogance
- Factual without filler
- Technical without jargon (explain when necessary)
- Specific without verbose

### What NOT to Do
- ❌ Avoid adjectives: "innovative," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class"
- ❌ Avoid filler: "proven," "thought leader," "passionate about," "dynamic"
- ❌ Avoid weak verbs: "leveraged," "spearheaded," "enabled"
- ❌ Avoid hedging: "helped," "contributed to," "was part of" (when sole responsibility)
- ❌ Avoid superlatives: "the best," "the most," "leading"

### What TO Do
- ✓ Use specific action verbs: architected, designed, built, led, launched, created, patented
- ✓ Use numbers: "7M+," "350%," "23.5 billion," "5M+ concurrent"
- ✓ Use technical terms precisely: "SSAI," "SCTE-35," "asynchronous workflows," "generative AI"
- ✓ Clarify scope: "individual contributor," "sole architect," "principal engineer," "head architect"
- ✓ Link to validation: Emmy Awards, patents (7 granted, 3 pending), scale metrics

## Resume Application Pattern

### Always Include
- Emmy Awards (all resumes, all formats)

### Situation-Task-Action-Result (STAR) Structure
When telling stories about accomplishments:
- **Situation:** Industry context or business problem
- **Task:** What specifically was required
- **Action:** What you personally did (technical approach, decisions made)
- **Result:** Measurable outcome (revenue, user scale, awards)

### Example STAR
- **S:** Peacock facing revenue challenges from limited interactive advertising; Key Plays driving engagement but pause ad inventory underutilized
- **T:** Design client-side pause ad system to generate unlimited ads without violating FreeWheel response size constraints
- **A:** Architected rolling ad call model (initial batch + trigger-based follow-ups); implemented client-side caching to meet latency requirements
- **R:** Overcame 10-ad FreeWheel limit; unlocked millions of additional ad impressions; cited as gold-standard implementation

## Consistency Checklist

Before finalizing any resume or communication:
- ☐ All action verbs are specific and active (no "leveraged," "spearheaded")
- ☐ All significant achievements include metrics or scale
- ☐ Individual vs. team contributions are clarified
- ☐ No vague adjectives ("innovative," "dynamic," "passionate")
- ☐ Technical terminology is precise and correctly used
- ☐ Emmy Awards are mentioned where relevant
- ☐ Sentence structure is direct (no unnecessary subordination)
- ☐ Tone is confident but not arrogant
